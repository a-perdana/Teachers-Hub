# Facilitator Conventions — Shared Playbook

This file is the shared standard for **every** Eduversal PD session. Each session guide assumes you have read it once. It is written for the facilitator, not for participants.

---

## 1. The rule that shapes everything: ESL / EAL

> **All Eduversal PD material is written and delivered in English — but always as ESL / EAL English.**

Our participants are Indonesian educators. They teach and lead in English, but English is an *additional* language for almost all of them — a second or third language, not their mother tongue.

- **ESL** = English as a Second Language.
- **EAL** = English as an Additional Language. This is the term Cambridge uses, and the one we prefer, because it does not assume English is only the *second* language — for many it is the third.

**This is not optional, and it is not only for handouts. It applies to every word in every document and every word you say:** facilitator talking points, slide bullets, vignettes, activity instructions, one-pagers, and workbooks. The *content* can be sophisticated; the *language* must be accessible.

**What ESL/EAL English looks like (the standard for the whole `docs/pd/` corpus):**

- **Short sentences.** One idea per sentence. Aim for CEFR **B1–B2** in participant-facing text. Cut every sentence you can split in two.
- **Active voice.** "The appraiser scores the lesson" — not "the lesson is scored by the appraiser."
- **Common words first.** "Use" not "utilise". "Show" not "demonstrate" (unless it is a defined term we are teaching). "Before" not "prior to".
- **Gloss every term the first time.** Do not say "formative assessment" and move on. Say "formative assessment — that means checking learning *during* the lesson, while you can still change something." Once glossed, you may use it plainly.
- **Define our acronyms on first use, every document.** F2, KPI, CTS, EAL — spell it out the first time it appears, even if it appeared in another guide.
- **Concrete over abstract.** Give an example before a definition. "When Bu Ratih asked Dina to explain her method…" lands before "elicitation techniques" does.
- **No idioms, no metaphors that depend on culture.** "Get the ball rolling", "low-hanging fruit", "on the same page" — replace with plain language.

**And when you deliver it live:**

- **Show, then tell.** Put the key word on a slide *before* you say it.
- **Give thinking time.** After a question, wait. Silence is normal. Counting to five in your head is not too long.
- **Let people talk in their first language in small groups,** then report back in English. Ideas first, language second.
- **Check understanding, not agreement.** "Does that make sense?" gets a polite yes from everyone. Instead ask: "Tell your partner, in your own words, what F2 measures."

> **Authoring note for anyone writing in `docs/pd/`:** the ESL/EAL rule above governs the *whole corpus*, not only participant handouts. Facilitator notes may carry more professional terms (the reader is a trainer), but they must still be glossed and kept in plain, short-sentence English. If a sentence needs a re-read to parse, rewrite it.

## 2. The shape of a session — timing blocks

Every Eduversal session, online or in person, follows the same five blocks. The minutes flex; the order does not.

| Block | Share of time | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Welcome & frame** | ~10% | Why are we here? What will you leave with? Set the tone. |
| **Input** | ~25% | The facilitator explains the idea. Short. Concrete. |
| **Activity** | ~40% | Participants *do* something — sort, score, plan, practise. This is where learning happens. |
| **Debrief** | ~15% | Surface what came up. Correct misunderstandings. Name the pattern. |
| **Close** | ~10% | One commitment each. Exit ticket. Where to find more. |

> **Why activity gets the most time:** PD that is all "input" (a person talking) rarely changes practice. The EEF (Effective PD, 2021) finds PD works when it builds technique through practice, not just knowledge through telling. So we keep the talking short and the doing long.

A 90-minute session is therefore roughly: 10 min welcome · 22 min input · 36 min activity · 14 min debrief · 8 min close. Each session guide gives the exact run-of-show.

## 3. Face-to-face vs online — the variants

Every activity in our guides has both a face-to-face and an online form. The principle is the same; the mechanics differ.

| | Face-to-face | Online |
|---|---|---|
| Small-group work | Tables of 4–5 | Breakout rooms of 3–4 |
| Sorting / ranking | Printed cards on a table | A shared slide or Jamboard / FigJam frame |
| Live scoring | Paper workbook + show of hands | Workbook on screen + poll (Mentimeter / Google Form / Zoom poll) |
| Reporting back | One person stands and shares | One person unmutes; others react with emoji first |
| Reading time | Hand out the one-pager | Share the link in chat; give the same quiet minutes |

**Online-specific rules:**
- Cameras on for discussion, optional during reading.
- Name the person before you ask a question ("Pak Andi, what did your group decide?") — open questions get silence online.
- Use the chat as a parallel channel. Quiet participants often type before they speak.
- Keep breakout rooms short and give a single, clear task. Re-read the task into the room before you send people.

## 4. Opening a session — quick frames (not childish icebreakers)

Adult professionals do not need a game. They need a reason to lean in. Pick one:

- **"One word."** Each person types/says one word for how they feel about [the topic] right now. Reveals the room's mood in 60 seconds.
- **"True for me."** Read 3 statements ("I have observed a colleague's lesson this year"). Hands up / green check for each. Shows shared experience.
- **"The question you brought."** Each person writes the one question they hope this session answers. Park them on a wall/slide; return to them at the close.

## 5. Closing a session — exit tickets

Never end with "any questions?" — that closes the room, it does not check it. Instead, every session ends with a written exit ticket. Standard form:

1. **One thing I now understand** that I did not before.
2. **One thing I will do differently** in the next two weeks.
3. **One thing I am still unsure about.**

Collect them (paper, or a Google Form for online). The third line tells you what to follow up. This is also our **post-session evidence** that the PD happened and landed.

## 6. The coaching stance (for any session where we give feedback)

Several Eduversal sessions ask leaders to give feedback — on lessons, on practice, on goals. The Eduversal feedback protocol is **Glow / Grow / Go** (built into Appraisal v2.1 — `appraisal-framework-v2.json` adds `feedback_prompts.grow` and `.go` to every scored item):

- **Glow** — name one specific strength. Evidence, not flattery. ("When you asked Dina to explain her method, three other students changed their answers — that is checking for understanding in action.")
- **Grow** — name one specific area, tied to evidence. ("In the last ten minutes, six students were not working. The task may have been finished too early.")
- **Go** — agree one concrete next step, with a date. ("Before next week, plan one extension task for early finishers. Bring it to our next chat.")

> **Why specific beats general:** Kraft, Blazar & Hogan (2018) — a meta-analysis of teacher coaching — finds coaching changes practice when it is specific and practice-focused, not when it is general praise or a long list. One Glow, one Grow, one Go. Not five of each.

## 7. Anchoring discipline (the Eduversal habit)

When a session makes a claim about a framework, the guide cites where that claim comes from. As a facilitator, you do not need to read participants the citation — but you should know it is there, because it means:

- You can answer "where does this come from?" with confidence.
- The content is current (the provenance lists the publish date of every standard).
- If a participant pushes back, you can show the source rather than defend an opinion.
