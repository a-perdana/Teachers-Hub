# Facilitator Session Guide — Understanding Your Appraisal (for Teachers)

> **Read `_facilitator-conventions.md` first** — especially §1 (ESL / EAL) and §2 (the five timing blocks).

---

## Why this session exists

Teachers often feel anxious about appraisal. They are not sure what is measured, who decides, or what the grade means for them. Anxiety makes appraisal feel like a test to survive, not a tool to grow.

This session removes the mystery. When a teacher knows exactly what the rubric looks for, appraisal stops being a threat and becomes useful. The teacher who has read the rubric before the observation walks in calm — and teaches better.

---

## Learning objectives

By the end of this session, each teacher can:

1. **Name** the four parts of the appraisal (F1–F4) and say how much each counts.
2. **Read** one F2 rubric and explain the difference between a "3" and a "4".
3. **Describe** the Glow / Grow / Go feedback they will receive.
4. **Say** how appraisal is different from KPI and Competency.

---

## Materials & setup

- The one-pager `onepager-how-appraisal-works.md` — one per teacher.
- The one-pager `../00-program-overview/three-rating-systems-at-a-glance.md` — one per teacher.
- One printed F2 rubric extract (use item D1.1 — Lesson Plan Structure — all four levels).
- Sticky notes (face-to-face) or a shared board (online) for the "questions you brought" frame.

**Face-to-face:** tables of 4–5; printed one-pagers.
**Online:** breakout rooms of 3–4; one-pagers shared as links; a shared slide for the rubric activity.

## Facilitator talking points

**On F2 being half.** *"Half of your appraisal comes from one observed lesson. That sounds like a lot of pressure. But it is also good news: it means the biggest part is your teaching — the thing you do every day — not your paperwork."* (`appraisal-framework-v2.json → weights.F2 = 0.5`).

**On F4 not counting.** *"Your students answer a survey. You will see it. It is useful. But it does not change your grade. We collect it to help you, not to judge you with it."* (`appraisal-framework-v2.json → weights.F4` is informative-only).

**On reading the rubric (the heart of the session).** Use real D1.1 text:
- **Level 3:** *"well-structured with clear sequencing and reasonable timing. Minor pacing gaps are present but do not undermine coherence."*
- **Level 4:** *"carefully structured sequence with logical progression and realistic time allocation for each phase. **Transitions between phases are explicitly planned.**"*

*"The difference is not 'better'. It is one specific thing: did you* ***plan how you move*** *from one part of the lesson to the next? That is something you can do tomorrow. The rubric does not ask you to be a genius. It asks for specific, doable things."* (`appraisal-framework-v2.json → F2 D1.1`; anchors to **CTS 3.2 — cambridge/teacher-standards-2023.json**).

**On the Cambridge backbone.** *"Every item in your appraisal points back to a Cambridge Teacher Standard. So this is not one school's opinion. It is the same standard used across Cambridge schools."*

## Activity instructions

### Read the rubric like an appraiser (22 min)
In pairs, teachers read all four levels of the D1.1 rubric (on the one-pager / a slide). Their task: **find the one phrase that moves a lesson from a 3 to a 4.** Then they answer: *"What could a teacher do, before the lesson, to reach the 4?"*

- **Online:** breakout rooms of 2–3; the rubric on a shared slide; report the phrase in the chat.

> **Why this works:** when teachers see that "4" is a *specific, plannable thing* (explicit transitions), the rubric stops being scary. They leave knowing one concrete action.

---

## Common questions (answer in simple English)

**"What if I have a bad day on the observation day?"**
Tell your appraiser. Observation is one lesson, but it is part of a bigger picture (F1, F3 too). And the feedback is meant to help, not to punish one bad day.

**"Can I see the rubric before the observation?"**
Yes. The rubric is not secret. Read it, plan with it, and ask your appraiser if anything is unclear.

**"Does my grade decide if I keep my job?"**
Each school decides how it uses appraisal. The appraisal *describes* your teaching and gives you feedback. Ask your school leader how your school uses it.

**"Is appraisal the same as my KPI?"**
No. KPI is your yearly targets (results). Appraisal is how you teach (practice). They are separate. See the three-systems one-pager.

## Exit ticket

1. One thing about appraisal I now understand better.
2. One thing I will do differently before my next observation.
3. One thing I am still unsure about.

> Collect line 3. The shared worries tell you what to follow up — and what to tell appraisers to explain more clearly.
