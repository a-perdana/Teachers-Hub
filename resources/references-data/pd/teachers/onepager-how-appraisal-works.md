# How Appraisal Works — for Teachers

**Appraisal** is the once-a-year look at *how you teach*. Its full name is the **Eduversal Teacher Appraisal v2.1**. This page explains it in one read.

> **In one sentence:** your appraiser watches one lesson and reviews your work, scores each part from **1 to 4**, and these become one letter grade from **A to F** — then you get feedback to help you grow.

---

## The four parts (and how much each counts)

Your appraisal has **four parts**. Three of them count toward your grade. One is for information only.

| Part | What it looks at | How much it counts |
|---|---|---|
| **F1 — Administration** | Your records, plans, and how you follow school systems. | 20% |
| **F2 — Class Observation** | Your actual teaching, watched in **one formal lesson** of 35–40 minutes. | **50% — the biggest part** |
| **F3 — Work Inspection** | A review of your work at the end-of-year meeting. | 20% |
| **F4 — Student Survey** | What your students say about your class. | 10% — **for information only; it does not change your grade** |

*Source: `appraisal-framework-v2.json` → weights.*

---

## The biggest part: F2 — Class Observation

Half of your grade comes from **one observed lesson**. Your appraiser watches you teach and scores **45 small points** across four areas:

- **Lesson Planning** — is the lesson well-designed and clearly sequenced?
- **Classroom Management** — is the room calm, safe, and ready to learn?
- **Instructional Process** — is the teaching clear, active, and engaging?
- **Assessment** — do you check learning during the lesson?

> **Good to know:** the appraiser scores what *happens in the lesson*, against a written guide called a **rubric** (a rubric describes exactly what a "1", "2", "3", or "4" looks like). They are not scoring whether they like you. They score the evidence — what they saw and heard.

---

## How the scores become a grade

Each point is scored **1 to 4**:

| Score | Means |
|---|---|
| **4** | Distinguished |
| **3** | Proficient |
| **2** | Basic |
| **1** | Unsatisfactory |

The scores combine into one number out of 100, which becomes a **letter band**:

**A** (Excellent, 86–100) · **B** (Good, 66–85) · **C** (Satisfactory, 51–65) · **D** (Needs Improvement, 36–50) · **F** (Unsatisfactory, 0–35).

*Source: `appraisal-framework-v2.json` → scoring_scale + predicate_bands.*

---

## The cycle, in four steps

1. **Before** — you and your appraiser agree a date. You prepare your lesson plan and your records.
2. **During** — the appraiser watches one formal lesson (35–40 minutes) and scores F2 live.
3. **After** — you meet to talk through F1 and F3, and look at the F4 student survey together.
4. **Feedback** — your appraiser gives you **Glow / Grow / Go**: one strength, one area to grow, and one clear next step with a date.

---

## What this means for you

- **It is not a trap.** The rubric is written down. You can read it before the observation. Nothing is secret.
- **The same standard applies to everyone.** Every item points back to a **Cambridge Teacher Standard** (for example, planning a clear lesson is CTS 3.2). Your appraisal uses the same lens as every other Eduversal teacher.
- **The goal is growth.** The grade is a snapshot. The feedback is the gift — one clear next step, not a long list.
- **Appraisal is one of three systems.** It is *not* the same as your KPI (your yearly targets) or your Competency level (your long-term growth). See *The Three Rating Systems at a Glance*.
