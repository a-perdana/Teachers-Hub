# Facilitator Session Guide — AI in the Classroom (for Teachers)

> **Read `_facilitator-conventions.md` first** — especially §1 (ESL / EAL) and §2 (the five timing blocks).

---

## Why this session exists

Teachers are already using AI — or worried they should be, or worried they should not. Two things are now true for Eduversal in 2026:

- **The Cambridge coursework line moved.** Undeclared, substantive AI use in coursework can void a qualification (Cambridge GenAI in Coursework Policy, 2024).
- **The data-protection line moved.** Typing a student's name into a free AI tool is now a regulated issue, not a personal choice (UU PDP No. 27/2022, fully enforceable since October 2024).

So teachers need a clear, practical answer: *Where does AI help me, where must I never use it, and how do I stay safe?* This session gives them two simple questions to ask, two quick wins they can use this week, and the red lines they must never cross.

> **Important boundary:** this session is about teachers using AI in *their professional work* (planning, drafting, differentiating). It is **not** about students using AI for graded work — that is a separate policy conversation owned by the Cambridge Coordinator.

---

## Learning objectives

By the end of this session, each teacher can:

1. **Ask** the two questions that decide if a task is safe for AI.
2. **Use** one quick-win workflow (lesson-plan skeleton or differentiation).
3. **Name** the red lines — especially never putting student data into public AI tools.
4. **Say** where AI fits in the new AI & Digital Literacy competency domain.

---

## Materials & setup

- A printed "Two Questions + Red Lines" handout (drawn from the AI Playbook — content below).
- The one-pager `../00-program-overview/three-rating-systems-at-a-glance.md` (to place the AI competency domain).
- **A device per teacher** if you want them to try a live prompt; otherwise a projected demo.
- The school's list of **approved AI tools** (ask your Academic Coordinator before the session — this matters).

**Face-to-face:** tables of 4–5; printed handout; one shared device per table for the demo if devices are limited.
**Online:** breakout rooms of 3–4; handout as a link; facilitator demos a live prompt on shared screen.

> **Before the session:** confirm which AI tools your school has approved. The data-protection rule depends on using an *approved* tool with a contract that protects student data. Do not run the live-prompt activity on a free personal account with any real student detail.

## Facilitator talking points

**On the frame: AI is a tool.** *"AI is a tool, not a colleague and not a teacher. You stay accountable for every decision in your classroom. AI is fast at pattern-shaped work — drafting, summarising, restructuring. It does not replace judgement — grading work that counts, deciding what a child needs, talking to a parent."* (`teacher-playbook.json → orientation`).

**The two questions (the core of the session).** *(`teacher-playbook.json → orientation.the_two_questions_to_always_ask_first`)*
- **Q1:** *"Does this task need my professional judgement, or is it pattern-shaped drafting?"* Drafting a lesson skeleton or differentiated practice = fair game. Grading a summative, judging Cambridge coursework authenticity = NOT.
- **Q2:** *"Would I be comfortable if a parent, a Cambridge moderator, or my principal saw the prompt I am about to type and the output I am about to use?"* If not, don't send it.

**The most important red line.** *"Never type a student's name, photo, ID, or records into a free or personal AI tool. Ever."* Show the example:
- **BAD:** *"Help me write a report comment for Andi Pratama, Year 8, who got 65% and has trouble focusing."*
- **GOOD:** *"Help me write a report comment for a Year 8 student who scored at the lower end and struggles with attention."* (no names, no specifics).

*"Indonesia's UU PDP — the personal data protection law — became fully enforceable in October 2024. The school is responsible for what you type. Use anonymous descriptions, and only school-approved tools."* (`redFlagsAndRedlines.json → rl_01_pii_in_public_ai`).

**On the Cambridge coursework line.** *"For work that counts toward a Cambridge qualification, undeclared substantive AI use can void it. When in doubt about coursework, the answer is never 'just try it' — ask your Cambridge Coordinator."* (Cambridge GenAI in Coursework Policy 2024; `competency_framework/teachers → aid-3`).

**On staying accountable.** *"AI gives you a draft. You give it judgement. Always review and edit. The skeleton saves you 20 minutes; spend them on the part only you can do — knowing where YOUR students will get stuck."* (`teacher-playbook.json → quickWins.qw_lesson_plan_skeleton`).

## Activity instructions

### Try one quick win (24 min)
Pick ONE for the room (or let pairs choose):

**Option A — Lesson-plan skeleton.** Using an approved tool, paste *anonymous* lesson context (subject, grade, Cambridge unit, lesson length, a generic class description like "a Year 8 class with 3 EAL students"). Ask for: objective options, a starter, a main activity scaffold, 3 formative-check questions, 2 differentiation moves, an exit ticket. Then **review and edit**. (`teacher-playbook.json → qw_lesson_plan_skeleton`.)

**Option B — Differentiation in 3 versions.** Take an existing practice set. Ask for an "access" version, an "on-level" version, and a "stretch" version. Stretch means *more thinking*, not just harder numbers. Then **review and edit**. (`teacher-playbook.json → qw_differentiated_practice`.)

The rule for both: **anonymous context only**, **approved tool only**, **always review the output**.

- **If devices are limited:** the facilitator demos on a shared screen and the room critiques the output together — what would you keep, what would you fix?
- **Online:** demo live; then breakout rooms try it and bring one output back to critique.

> **Why this works:** teachers leave having *done* one safe, useful thing — not just heard a warning. The quick win builds confidence; the anonymous-context rule builds the safe habit at the same time.

## Common questions (answer in simple English)

**"Is it cheating to use AI to plan my lessons?"**
No. Planning support is pattern-shaped drafting (Q1). You review and adapt every output. The judgement stays yours. That is good professional use.

**"Which tool should I use?"**
Only a school-approved tool. Ask your Academic Coordinator. Approved tools have a contract that protects student data; free personal accounts do not.

**"Can my students use AI for their work?"**
That is a separate policy, owned by your Cambridge Coordinator, and it is strict for work that counts. This session is about *your* professional use. For students, check the coursework policy first.

**"I have never used AI. Am I behind?"**
No. "Awareness" is an honest and real starting level in the AI & Digital Literacy domain. Start with one quick win, used safely. That is enough for now.

## Exit ticket

1. One task where I will use AI safely this term.
2. One red line I will always remember.
3. One thing I am still unsure about.

> Collect line 3 — uncertainty about AI is high, and the shared questions tell you (and the Cambridge Coordinator) what to clarify next.
