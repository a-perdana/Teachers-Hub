C4.1 / E4.1 — Geometrical Terms & Vocabulary

📚 Core + Extended 🎯 Lines, angles, shapes, solids, circles
0 correct

🎚️ Difficulty
👩‍🏫 Teacher Panel — Geometrical Terms
❌ Common Misconceptions
  • Confusing diameter with radius (diameter = 2 × radius)
  • Calling any four-sided shape a "rectangle"
  • Thinking "reflex" means "obtuse"
  • Confusing arc, chord and tangent in circles
  • Forgetting that a sector is a "pizza-slice" (bounded by two radii + arc)
✅ Teaching Strategies
  • Flashcard matching: term → diagram → definition
  • Draw a circle and label all 7 parts in one go
  • Hierarchy chart: quadrilaterals → parallelograms → rectangles
  • Real-life linking: tangent = tyre touching road at one point
  • Use colour coding for each family of shapes
📝 Worked Example
Q: State the mathematical name for a solid with two circular faces and one curved surface.
A: Cylinder. The two flat faces are circles (top and bottom) and the lateral surface is curved.

📖 Key Definitions

Angles: acute < 90° · right = 90° · obtuse 90°–180° · reflex 180°–360°

Parallel
Lines that never meet; same direction. Marked with arrows (→→).
Perpendicular
Lines that meet at exactly 90°.
Vertex (pl. vertices)
A corner point where two or more edges meet.
Similar
Same shape, different size. Corresponding angles equal; sides in proportion.
Congruent
Exactly the same shape and size.
Chord
A straight line joining two points on a circle (not passing through centre).
Tangent
A straight line that touches a circle at exactly one point.
Sector
Region bounded by two radii and an arc ("pizza slice").
Segment
Region between a chord and the arc it cuts off.
Arc
Part of the circumference. Extended: major arc (larger) vs minor arc (smaller).
Extended only
Plane
A flat 2D surface extending infinitely.
Perpendicular Bisector
A line that cuts another line in half at 90°.
Hemisphere
Half a sphere, cut by a flat circular face.
Frustum
A cone or pyramid with its top cut off by a plane parallel to the base.
Major / Minor Arc
Major arc = larger part of circle; minor arc = smaller part.

C4.2 / E4.2 — Constructions & Nets

📚 Core + Extended 🎯 Measuring, triangle construction, nets
0 correct
0 streak
0% score
🎚️ Difficulty
👩‍🏫 Teacher Panel — Constructions
❌ Common Misconceptions
  • Using a protractor incorrectly (wrong scale: 60° vs 120°)
  • Forgetting that SAS means the angle is between the two sides
  • Drawing nets that don't fold up (faces in wrong positions)
  • Not keeping compass width fixed when constructing triangles
✅ Teaching Strategies
  • Let students cut and fold paper nets — tactile reinforcement
  • Stress: measure angles from the correct baseline on a protractor
  • For triangle construction, always sketch first with labels
  • Net puzzles: which of these folds into a cube?
📝 Worked Example
Q: Construct triangle ABC where AB = 6 cm, BC = 4 cm, and angle ABC = 55°.
Steps:
1. Draw AB = 6 cm.
2. At B, measure and draw an angle of 55° from BA.
3. Along the new ray from B, mark BC = 4 cm to get C.
4. Join A to C to complete the triangle.

📖 Construction Cases
SSS
Given all 3 sides. Draw base, then arc from each end; intersection = third vertex.
SAS
Two sides and the included angle between them. Draw base, use protractor for angle, mark second side.
ASA / AAS
One side and two angles. Draw the side, construct angles at each end; intersection = third vertex.
Net
A flat shape that folds to form a 3D solid. All faces must be present; adjacent faces share an edge.

🧭 Scale Drawings & Bearings

C4.3 / E4.3 — Scale Drawings & Three-Figure Bearings

📚 Core + Extended 🎯 Scale factors, map reading, compass bearings
0 correct
0 streak
0% score
🎚️ Difficulty
👩‍🏫 Teacher Panel — Scale Drawings & Bearings
❌ Common Misconceptions
  • Writing a bearing as 45° instead of 045° (must be 3 digits)
  • Measuring bearing from east instead of north
  • Measuring bearing anticlockwise instead of clockwise
  • Confusing map scale: "1 : 50 000" means 1 cm = 500 m, NOT 50 000 cm
  • Not converting units before applying scale
✅ Teaching Strategies
  • Always draw a North arrow first, then measure clockwise
  • Practise with real OS maps or Google Maps screenshots
  • Mnemonics: "bearings start North, go clockwise, always 3 digits"
  • Scale drawing: always state the scale used
  • Back-bearing: add/subtract 180° to find return bearing
📝 Worked Example
Q: A map has scale 1 : 25 000. A road measures 8 cm on the map. Find the real length in km.
A: Real = 8 × 25 000 cm = 200 000 cm = 2000 m = 2 km.
📋 Syllabus Coverage
CodeStatementLevel
C4.3aDraw and interpret scale drawingsCore
C4.3bUse three-figure bearings (000° to 360°), measured clockwise from northCore
E4.3Same as Core — solve problems involving bearings and scale drawings with greater complexityExtended
📖 Key Rules
Bearings rule: always measured clockwise from North, always written as three digits (e.g. 045°, 090°, 270°).
Scale formula: Real distance = Map distance × Scale factor (convert units as needed).
Cardinal directions as bearings:
North = 000° · East = 090° · South = 180° · West = 270°

Back-bearing: If bearing from A to B is θ, bearing from B to A = θ + 180° (if θ < 180°) or θ − 180° (if θ ≥ 180°).

🔁 Similarity

C4.4 / E4.4 — Similar Shapes & Solids

📚 Core + Extended 🎯 Scale factor, length/area/volume ratios
0 correct
0 streak
0% score
🎚️ Difficulty
👩‍🏫 Teacher Panel — Similarity
❌ Common Misconceptions
  • Multiplying by scale factor for area (should square it)
  • Multiplying by scale factor for volume (should cube it)
  • Confusing "similar" with "congruent"
  • Using the wrong ratio direction (A:B vs B:A)
  • Not checking all three pairs of angles when proving similarity
✅ Teaching Strategies
  • Teach as a hierarchy: length → area → volume (×k, ×k², ×k³)
  • Use real-life scaling: maps, model aeroplanes, photocopying
  • Draw both shapes labelled so students identify correct matching sides
  • For proof of similarity: state all three angle equalities with reasons
📝 Worked Example
Q: Two similar cylinders have radii 3 cm and 6 cm. If the volume of the small one is 84 cm³, find the volume of the large one.
A: Scale factor k = 6/3 = 2. Volume ratio = k³ = 8. Volume of large = 84 × 8 = 672 cm³.
📋 Syllabus Coverage
CodeStatementLevel
C4.4Calculate lengths of similar shapes given a scale factorCore
E4.4aUse the relationships between lengths, areas and volumes of similar shapes and solids: Area ratio = k², Volume ratio = k³Extended

📖 Key Rules
Length scale factor k: If two similar shapes have corresponding lengths in ratio k,
then Area ratio = k² and Volume ratio = k³.

Proving triangles similar (Extended):
AA — two pairs of equal angles
SAS — two sides in proportion and the included angle equal
SSS — all three sides in proportion

Extended only

To find missing length: set up ratio → cross-multiply → solve.
To find area/volume: identify k first from lengths, then apply k² or k³.
Reverse: if areas given, k = √(A₁/A₂); if volumes given, k = ∛(V₁/V₂).

0 correct

🎚️ Difficulty
👩‍🏫 Teacher Panel — Symmetry
❌ Common Misconceptions
  • A rectangle has 4 lines of symmetry (it has only 2)
  • Confusing order of rotational symmetry with number of sides
  • Thinking a parallelogram has line symmetry (it does not)
  • Stating order 1 when a shape has no rotational symmetry (should say "none" or order 1)
✅ Teaching Strategies
  • Use tracing paper — fold/rotate to check symmetry physically
  • Systematic table: shape → lines of symmetry → rotational order
  • For 3D: hold solid and count planes of symmetry by inspection
  • Link to regular polygons: n-gon has n lines and order n
📝 Worked Example
Q: State the number of lines of symmetry and the order of rotational symmetry of a regular hexagon.
A: Lines of symmetry: 6. Order of rotational symmetry: 6 (rotations of 60°, 120°, 180°, 240°, 300°, 360° all map it onto itself).

📐 Angles

C4.6 / E4.6 — Angle Properties & Polygons

📚 Core + Extended 🎯 Angle rules, parallel lines, polygon angle sums
0 correct
0 streak
0% score
🎚️ Difficulty
👩‍🏫 Teacher Panel — Angles
❌ Common Misconceptions
  • Using "F angles" / "Z angles" in explanations (not accepted — use "corresponding" / "alternate")
  • Confusing co-interior (same side, supplementary) with alternate (opposite sides, equal)
  • Applying interior angle sum to irregular polygons incorrectly
  • Forgetting: exterior angles of ANY polygon always sum to 360°
  • Assuming interior angle = 360°/n (only for REGULAR polygons)
✅ Teaching Strategies
  • Require full geometric reason in every angle calculation
  • Colour-code: corresponding angles (F-shape), alternate angles (Z-shape), co-interior (C/U-shape)
  • Walk through exterior angles of a polygon physically — one full turn = 360°
  • Formula chain: exterior = 360°/n → interior = 180° − exterior
📝 Worked Example
Q: Find the interior angle of a regular pentagon.
A: Sum of interior angles = (5 − 2) × 180° = 540°. Each interior angle = 540° ÷ 5 = 108°.
Or: exterior angle = 360°/5 = 72°; interior = 180° − 72° = 108°.

📖 Angle Rules Summary
Angles at a point
Sum = 360°
Angles on a straight line
Sum = 180°
Vertically opposite
Equal angles formed by two intersecting lines
Corresponding angles
Equal; same position at each parallel-line crossing (F-pattern)
Alternate angles
Equal; opposite sides of transversal between parallel lines (Z-pattern)
Co-interior angles
Sum = 180°; same side of transversal between parallel lines (C/U-pattern)
Interior angle sum of polygon: S = (n − 2) × 180°
Each interior angle (regular): = S ÷ n
Each exterior angle (regular): = 360° ÷ n

⭕ Circle Theorems I

C4.7 / E4.7 — Circle Angle Theorems

📚 Core + Extended 🎯 Angle in semicircle, tangent-radius, centre-circumference, cyclic quadrilateral, alternate segment
0 correct
0 streak
0% score
🎚️ Difficulty
👩‍🏫 Teacher Panel — Circle Theorems I
❌ Common Misconceptions
  • Thinking angle at circumference = angle at centre (should be half)
  • Forgetting OA = OB (radii) when using isosceles triangles in proofs
  • Alternate segment: confusing which angle is in the alternate segment
  • Cyclic quadrilateral: adding adjacent angles instead of opposite
  • Not stating the theorem name as the reason in proofs
✅ Teaching Strategies
  • Dynamic GeoGebra: drag circumference point to see angle at centre always double
  • Colour-code theorems; students build their own theorem booklet
  • Always mark O (centre) clearly and identify radii for isosceles sub-triangles
  • Alternate segment: draw tangent in colour; the angle between tangent and chord = angle in far segment
📝 Worked Example
Q: O is the centre of a circle. Angle AOB = 130°. Find the angle at the circumference subtended by the same arc AB, on the major arc side.
A: The angle at the circumference = ½ × angle at centre = ½ × 130° = 65° (angle at centre theorem).

📖 Theorem Reference
Angle in a semicircle: If AB is a diameter and P is on the circle, then angle APB = 90°.
Tangent–radius: The angle between a tangent and a radius at the point of tangency = 90°.
Extended theorems
Angle at centre: Angle AOB = 2 × angle APB, where O is centre and P is any point on the major arc.
Angles in same segment: All angles subtended by the same chord on the same side are equal.
Cyclic quadrilateral: Opposite angles sum to 180°. (∠A + ∠C = 180°, ∠B + ∠D = 180°)
Alternate segment: The angle between the tangent at P and chord PQ equals the angle QPR where R is any point in the alternate segment.

⭕ Circle Theorems II

E4.8 — Chords, Tangents & Further Circle Properties

📚 Extended Only 🎯 Equal chords, perpendicular bisector of chord, equal tangents
0 correct
0 streak
0% score
🎚️ Difficulty
👩‍🏫 Teacher Panel — Circle Theorems II
❌ Common Misconceptions
  • Forgetting that equal chords are equidistant from centre (not just parallel)
  • Not using Pythagoras when finding distances from centre to chord
  • Confusing perpendicular bisector of chord with perpendicular from centre to chord (same thing — but context matters)
  • Thinking tangent lengths from external point differ
✅ Teaching Strategies
  • Proof of perpendicular bisector theorem: use congruent triangles (SSS or RHS)
  • Equal tangent lengths: draw both tangents and use Pythagoras to verify
  • Always mark right angles when perpendicular from centre to chord is drawn
  • Kite formed by two tangent lines from external point — good geometry puzzle
📝 Worked Example
Q: Two tangents from external point T touch a circle of radius 5 cm at points A and B. The distance OT = 13 cm. Find the length of each tangent.
A: OA ⊥ TA (tangent–radius). By Pythagoras: TA² = OT² − OA² = 169 − 25 = 144. TA = 12 cm.
📋 Syllabus Coverage
CodeTheoremLevel

📖 Theorem Reference
Perpendicular from centre bisects chord: If ON ⊥ AB (chord), then AN = NB.
Equal chords theorem: If chord AB = chord CD, then distance from O to AB = distance from O to CD.
Equal tangents from external point: If TA and TB are tangents from T to circle with centre O, then TA = TB.

Key technique: When perpendicular from centre O meets chord at midpoint N, triangle ONB is right-angled. Apply Pythagoras: OB² = ON² + NB².
