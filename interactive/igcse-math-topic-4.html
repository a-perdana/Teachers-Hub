👩‍🏫 Teacher Panel — Geometrical Terms
❌ Common Misconceptions
  • Confusing diameter with radius (diameter = 2 × radius)
  • Calling any four-sided shape a "rectangle"
  • Thinking "reflex" means "obtuse"
  • Confusing arc, chord and tangent
  • Forgetting sector = "pizza slice" (two radii + arc)
✅ Teaching Strategies
  • Flashcard matching: term → diagram → definition
  • Draw circle and label all 7 parts at once
  • Hierarchy chart: quadrilaterals → parallelograms → rectangles
  • Real-life: tangent = tyre touching road at one point
  • Colour-code each family of shapes
📝 Worked Example
Q: State the name for a solid with two circular faces and one curved surface.
A: Cylinder. Two flat circular faces (top and bottom), one lateral curved surface.

📖 Key Definitions

Angles: acute < 90° · right = 90° · obtuse 90°–180° · reflex 180°–360°

Parallel
Lines that never meet; marked with arrows (→→).
Perpendicular
Lines meeting at exactly 90°.
Vertex (pl. vertices)
Corner point where edges meet.
Similar
Same shape, different size. Angles equal; sides in proportion.
Congruent
Exactly same shape and size.
Chord
Straight line joining two points on a circle.
Tangent
Line touching a circle at exactly one point.
Sector
Region bounded by two radii and an arc ("pizza slice").
Segment
Region between a chord and the arc it cuts off.
Arc
Part of the circumference. Major arc = larger; minor = smaller.
Extended only
Plane
Flat 2D surface extending infinitely.
Perpendicular Bisector
Cuts a line segment in half at 90°.
Hemisphere
Half a sphere, cut by a flat circular face.
Frustum
A cone or pyramid with its top sliced off parallel to the base. (Volume calculations: Topic 5 — Mensuration)

📝 Worked Example — SAS Construction
Q: Construct triangle ABC where AB = 6 cm, BC = 4 cm, ∠ABC = 55°.
1. Draw AB = 6 cm.
2. At B, measure 55° from BA.
3. Along new ray from B, mark BC = 4 cm.
4. Join A to C to complete the triangle.

📖 Construction Cases Reference
SSS — Three sides
Draw base. Arc from each end; intersection = third vertex. Use compasses only.
SAS — Two sides + included angle
Draw base. Use protractor for angle at one end. Mark second side length along ray.
ASA — Side + two angles
Draw the known side. Construct the given angle at each end; rays intersect at third vertex.
AAS — Two angles + non-included side
Calculate third angle (180° − sum). Construct ASA with known side and any two angles.
Net
Flat shape that folds to form a 3D solid. All faces present; adjacent faces share an edge.

🔬 Lab 3 — Step-by-Step Construction Guide

Click each accordion to see the steps. Mark steps as you complete them physically.

SSS Construction ▼
  1. Draw the longest side AB as your base line.
  2. Set compass to length AC. Place point at A, draw arc above base.
  3. Set compass to length BC. Place point at B, draw arc above base.
  4. Mark the intersection of the two arcs as vertex C.
  5. Join A–C and B–C. Label all sides.
SAS Construction ▼
  1. Draw side AB = given length.
  2. Place protractor at B. Measure and mark the included angle.
  3. Draw a ray from B along the angle direction.
  4. Mark point C on the ray at the given length BC.
  5. Join A to C. Label the triangle.
ASA Construction ▼
  1. Draw the known side AB.
  2. At A, use protractor to construct the angle at A. Draw ray.
  3. At B, use protractor to construct the angle at B. Draw ray.
  4. The two rays intersect at vertex C.
  5. Label the triangle and measure any unknown sides.

C4.3 / E4.3 — Scale Drawings & Three-Figure Bearings

📚 Core + Extended🎯 Scale, map reading, compass bearings
0 correct
0 streak
0% score
🎚️ Difficulty
👩‍🏫 Teacher Panel — Scale & Bearings
❌ Common Misconceptions
  • Bearings measured from south or east (must be from north, clockwise)
  • Forgetting to give three figures (e.g. "65°" instead of "065°")
  • Back-bearing: adding 180° without checking if result > 360°
  • Confusing map scale 1:50 000 with 1 cm = 50 m
✅ Teaching Strategies
  • Compass mnemonics: Never Eat Shredded Wheat (N E S W)
  • Back-bearing rule: ±180° (add if <180°, subtract if ≥180°)
  • Scale drawing: measure in mm for accuracy, convert to cm
  • Real maps activity: measure road distances using scale
📝 Worked Example — Back Bearing
Q: Bearing from A to B is 065°. Find bearing from B to A.
A: Since 065° < 180°, add 180°: back-bearing = 065° + 180° = 245°.

📖 Bearing Rules
Three-figure bearing: Always measured from North, clockwise, written with 3 digits (e.g. 065°, 270°).
Back-bearing: If bearing AB = θ, then bearing BA = θ + 180° (if θ < 180°) or θ − 180° (if θ ≥ 180°).
North = 000°
Due north. Straight up on a map.
East = 090°
Quarter turn clockwise from North.
South = 180°
Half turn from North.
West = 270°
Three-quarter turn clockwise.

📚 Core + Extended🎯 Scale factor k, area ratio k², volume ratio k³
0 correct
0 streak

🎚️ Difficulty
👩‍🏫 Teacher Panel — Similarity
❌ Common Misconceptions
  • Using k² for length (should be k)
  • Forgetting area scales as k², volume as k³
  • Not identifying corresponding sides before forming ratio
  • Confusing similarity with congruence
✅ Teaching Strategies
  • Match corresponding vertices using equal angles first
  • State k = larger ÷ smaller explicitly
  • Area ratio: square k — "double k → quadruple area"
  • Volume: k=2 gives 8× volume; k=3 gives 27×
📝 Worked Example
Q: Shorter sides of two similar triangles: 3 cm and 7.5 cm. Find k and area ratio.
A: k = 7.5 ÷ 3 = 2.5. Area ratio = 2.5² = 6.25.

C4.6 / E4.6 — Angles: Lines, Polygons & Parallel Lines

📚 Core + Extended🎯 Parallel lines, polygon angles, multi-step problems
0 correct
0 streak
0% score
🎚️ Difficulty
👩‍🏫 Teacher Panel — Angles
❌ Common Misconceptions
  • Co-interior angles equal (they are supplementary)
  • Alternate angles not equal (they ARE equal)
  • Interior sum: using wrong formula
  • Exterior: adding instead of dividing 360°
✅ Teaching Strategies
  • F-angles (corresponding) = F shape between parallels
  • Z-angles (alternate) = Z/N shape
  • C-angles (co-interior) = C/U shape, sum 180°
  • Polygon sum: (n−2)×180°, draw diagonals to triangulate
📝 Worked Example
Q: AB∥CD. Angle ABE = 55°. Find angle BED (co-interior).
A: Co-interior sum = 180°. BED = 180 − 55 = 125°.

Vertically Opposite
Equal angles when two lines cross.

Regular interior angle

⭕ Circle Theorems I

C4.7 / E4.7 — Circle Angle Theorems

📚 Core + Extended🎯 Semicircle, centre-circumference, cyclic quad, alternate segment, tangent-radius
0 correct
0 streak
0% score
🎚️ Difficulty
👩‍🏫 Teacher Panel — Circle Theorems I
❌ Common Misconceptions
  • Angle at centre = angle at circumference (should be double)
  • Confusing "same segment" with "same arc"
  • Cyclic quad: opposite angles sum to 180° (not equal)
  • Alternate segment: confusing which angle is "in" the segment
✅ Teaching Strategies
  • Draw all 6 theorems on one circle diagram with colour coding
  • State the theorem in words before applying it
  • Cyclic quad: stress "opposite" not "adjacent"
  • Alternate segment: tangent and chord create the key angle
📝 All 6 Theorems Summary
1. Angle in semicircle = 90° (angle subtended by diameter at circumference)
2. Angle at centre = 2 × angle at circumference (same arc)
3. Angles in same segment are equal
4. Cyclic quadrilateral: opposite angles sum to 180°
5. Alternate segment theorem: angle between tangent and chord = angle in alternate segment
6. Tangent-radius: tangent is perpendicular to radius at point of contact

📖 Circle Theorems Quick Reference
T1 — Angle in semicircle: Angle subtended by a diameter at the circumference = 90°.
T2 — Centre-circumference: Angle at centre = 2 × angle at circumference (same arc).
T3 — Same segment: Angles subtended by the same arc at the circumference are equal.
T4 — Cyclic quadrilateral: Opposite angles sum to 180°.
T5 — Alternate segment: Angle between tangent and chord = angle in the alternate segment.
T6 — Tangent-radius: The tangent at any point is perpendicular to the radius at that point.

E4.8 — Chords, Tangents & Advanced Theorems

📚 Extended only🎯 Chord bisector, equal tangents, Pythagoras with circles
0 correct
0 streak
0% score
🎚️ Difficulty
👩‍🏫 Teacher Panel — Circle Theorems II
❌ Common Misconceptions
  • Chord length = 2 × distance from centre (wrong — use Pythagoras)
  • Two tangents from external point: forgetting they're equal length
  • Perpendicular from centre bisects chord — students reverse this
✅ Teaching Strategies
  • Draw the right-angle triangle: radius, perpendicular, half-chord
  • Equal tangents: draw OT and the two tangent points — isoceles triangle
  • Always label r, d (distance), and ½chord before applying Pythagoras
📝 Chord Length Formula
For a chord at distance d from centre, radius r:
Half-chord = √(r² − d²)
Full chord = 2√(r² − d²)
📋 Syllabus Coverage

📖 Key Theorems
Chord-Bisector: The perpendicular from the centre to a chord bisects the chord. Use: half-chord = √(r² − d²).
Equal Tangents: From an external point T, tangents to a circle at A and B satisfy TA = TB.
Tangent-Chord Angle: Angle between tangent and chord = angle in the alternate segment.
