❌ Misconception 1: "1 is a prime number." — A prime must have exactly two distinct factors (1 and itself). 1 has only one.
❌ Misconception 2: Choosing composite factors (e.g. "24 = 4 × 6"). Push students: "Is your factor prime? Keep splitting!"
❌ Misconception 3: Confusing factors and multiples. A factor of 24 must divide 24 exactly — no remainder.
✅ Teaching Strategy: After the tree, extend immediately to HCF/LCM using index notation. Ask: "Which primes appear in both?"

          ❌ Misconception 1: Rounding to nearest 10 when 1 significant figure is needed. E.g. 48.3 → 50 (nearest 10) but 1 sf = 50 too here — ask: what about 312? 1 sf = 300, not 310.
          ❌ Misconception 2: Computing the exact answer and calling it an estimate. Emphasise: round FIRST, then calculate with the rounded values.

            ⚡ Indices Lab
            0 pts
            👨‍🏫 Teacher Notes — Indices Lab
            ❌ Misconception 1: aᵐ + aⁿ = aᵐ⁺ⁿ — the most common error. Index laws apply to multiplication only, never addition. e.g. 2³ + 2² = 8 + 4 = 12, not 2⁵ = 32.
            ❌ Misconception 2: Negative index = negative number. a⁻ⁿ means the reciprocal 1/aⁿ — the result is positive (for positive a). 2⁻³ = 1/8, not −8.
            ❌ Misconception 3: (aᵐ)ⁿ = aᵐ⁺ⁿ — confusing the power rule with multiplication rule. Raise a power to a power → multiply indices.
            ✅ Teaching Strategy: Always expand first: 2³ × 2² = (2×2×2) × (2×2) = 2⁵. Let students see why the rule works before applying it abstractly.

              👨‍🏫 Teacher Notes — Number Types Explorer
              ❌ Misconception 1: "1 is a prime number." — Primes must have exactly two distinct factors. 1 has only one factor (itself), so it is neither prime nor composite.
              ❌ Misconception 2: "√2 is rational." — Irrational numbers cannot be written as p/q where p, q are integers. √2 = 1.41421356… never repeats and never terminates.
              ❌ Misconception 3: Confusing integers and natural numbers. Natural numbers start at 1 (or 0 in some conventions) — they exclude negatives. Integers include all negatives, zero, and positives.
              ✅ Teaching Strategy: Use the nested set diagram: ℕ ⊂ ℤ ⊂ ℚ ⊂ ℝ. Ask: "Can you place this number on the number line? Is it a fraction? Does it terminate or recur?"
