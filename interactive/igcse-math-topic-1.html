                    ❌ Misconception 1: Writing 0.3̄ as 0.3 (terminating). Stress the dot/bar notation — 0.3̄ = 0.333… is an infinite decimal, not 3 tenths.
                    ❌ Misconception 2: Multiplying by 10 regardless of how many digits recur. Two recurring digits → ×100; one → ×10. The rule: multiply so the recurring block lines up and cancels.

                            👨‍🏫 Teacher Notes — Surds
                            ❌ Misconception 1: "√8 = 4" — students confuse squaring and square-rooting. √8 ≈ 2.83, not 4. Stress: √8 = √(4×2) = 2√2.
                            ❌ Misconception 2: Leaving surds unsimplified (e.g. √12 instead of 2√3). Insist on finding the largest square factor — spotting 4 in 12 rather than just 2.
                            ❌ Misconception 3: Rationalising as 1/√2 = 1/2 (squaring numerator too). Remind: multiply numerator AND denominator by √2 → √2/2.
                            ❌ Misconception 4: Adding surds incorrectly: √2 + √3 ≠ √5. Surds can only be combined if they have the same radicand (like terms).
                            ✅ Teaching Strategy: Make a list of perfect squares (1,4,9,16,25,36,49,64,81,100). For any surd √n, ask: "What's the largest perfect square that divides n?" That factor comes outside the root.
                            ✅ Extension: (a+√b)(a−√b) = a²−b — difference of two squares eliminates the surd. This underpins conjugate rationalisation for expressions like 1/(1+√2).
                            √ Surds
                            A surd is an irrational root that cannot be simplified to a rational number (e.g. √2, √3, √5).
                            We simplify surds by pulling out the largest perfect-square factor, and rationalise denominators by multiplying by the surd.
                            Simplify:  √12 = √(4×3) = √4 × √3 = 2√3
                            Rationalise:  1/√3 = (1×√3)/(√3×√3) = √3/3 = √3/3

                                    📏 Limits of Accuracy
                                    0 pts
                                    👨‍🏫 Teacher Notes — Limits of Accuracy
                                    ❌ Misconception 1: Lower bound = value − rounding unit. Students write LB = 3.4 for 3.5 rounded to 1 dp. Correct: LB = 3.45 (half a unit below).
                                    ❌ Misconception 2: Upper bound is included. UB = 3.55 is a strict upper bound — no measured value can equal it (it would round to 3.6). UB is excluded from the interval.
                                    ❌ Misconception 3: Combining bounds: max of a sum = UB₁ + UB₂. Max of a product = UB₁ × UB₂. Max of a quotient = UB_numerator ÷ LB_denominator.
                                    ✅ Teaching Strategy: Number line diagrams. Draw the rounding interval and shade the half-unit either side. The closed dot is LB, the open dot is UB.
                                    📏 What Are Bounds?
                                    A measurement rounded to a given accuracy has an error interval:
                                    LB (lower bound) = value − ½ × rounding unit  |  UB (upper bound) = value + ½ × rounding unit
                                    Written as: LB ≤ x < UB  (LB is included, UB is excluded)

                                                                                      Direct: y ∝ x → y = kx. As x doubles, y doubles. k = y/x (constant).
                                                                                      Inverse: y ∝ 1/x → y = k/x. As x doubles, y halves. k = xy (constant).

                                                                                            0 pts
                                                                                            👨‍🏫 Teacher Notes — Exponential Growth & Decay
                                                                                            ❌ Misconception 1: Using P×r×n (simple interest) instead of P×rⁿ (compound). Simple grows linearly; exponential grows multiplicatively each period.
                                                                                            ❌ Misconception 2: Confusing growth multiplier (e.g. 1.05) with decay multiplier (e.g. 0.95). Growth > 1, decay < 1. Both are positive.
                                                                                            ❌ Misconception 3: Adding percentages: "5% for 3 years = 15% total." Wrong — compound: 1.05³ = 1.1576 → 15.76%, not 15%.
                                                                                            ✅ Strategy: Write multiplier first. Growth: m = 1 + r/100. Decay: m = 1 − r/100. Final = P × mⁿ. Always build a year-by-year table to verify.
                                                                                            📈 The Exponential Model
                                                                                            Exponential change applies the same multiplier every period — giving a curve, not a straight line.
                                                                                            📈 Growth
                                                                                            Final = P × (1 + r/100)ⁿ
                                                                                            Multiplier m > 1

                                                                                            Example: £2 000 at 3% for 4 yrs
                                                                                            m = 1.03
                                                                                            = 2000 × 1.03⁴
                                                                                            = 2000 × 1.12551
                                                                                            = £2 251.02
                                                                                            📉 Decay
                                                                                            Final = P × (1 − r/100)ⁿ
                                                                                            Multiplier m < 1

                                                                                            Example: Car £12 000, −8%/yr, 3 yrs
                                                                                            m = 0.92
                                                                                            = 12000 × 0.92³
                                                                                            = 12000 × 0.77869
                                                                                            = £9 344.26

                                                                                                0 pts
                                                                                                👨‍🏫 Teacher Notes — Algebraic Proportion
                                                                                                ❌ Misconception 1: Writing y = kx without finding k. Students stop at the proportionality statement and never substitute to find the constant.
                                                                                                ❌ Misconception 2: Confusing direct (more x → more y) with inverse (more x → less y). Use a table of values to test which fits.
                                                                                                ❌ Misconception 3: y ∝ x² — "double x → double y". Wrong: double x → quadruple y (k(2x)² = 4kx²). The scale factor must be squared.
                                                                                                ✅ 3-step method: (1) Write the statement y = k·f(x). (2) Substitute known x and y to find k. (3) Write the equation and substitute the new x.
