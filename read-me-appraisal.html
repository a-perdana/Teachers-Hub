This page explains the Appraisal section of your Admin menu. An appraisal is your once-a-year teaching review. You rate yourself first on Teacher Appraisal; your school leader observes you and agrees a final score; you read it on Appraisal Results.
For: Subject Teachers & Subject Leaders 2 pages: Teacher Appraisal · Results Eduversal Appraisal Framework v2.1 Anchored to Cambridge Teacher Standards 2023
Appraisal is the yearly "how is my teaching" review. It is separate from KPI (the numbers system) and the Competency Framework (your self-paced skill growth). Each is scored on its own — they never feed each other.

The Appraisal section in the Admin menu has 2 pages. An appraisal is a yearly review of your teaching. You rate yourself first; your school leader watches some of your lessons and agrees a final score; then you both talk it through. This page explains the whole idea in plain English. If a word looks new, Section 6 — Glossary explains it.

A short promise
The appraisal is meant to support your growth, not to catch you out. Your self-appraisal is your voice in the conversation. The score is agreed with you, after a discussion — never sprung on you. Come back here if you get stuck.
📏
What does it measure?
Your teaching against the Eduversal Appraisal Framework — a shared set of teaching focus areas, each rated 1–4. The scores combine into one composite and a clear A–F band.
👥
Who uses it?
Every teacher does a self-appraisal. Your school principal (the appraiser) does the observation and writes the official version on Academic Hub. You read the final result here on Teachers Hub.
One important separation. Appraisal is the yearly judgement system. It does not use your KPI numbers, and it does not feed your Competency Framework growth. They stay separate so each tells its own honest story. See Section 6 for the words.

Two pages. Here is exactly what each one is for, when to use it, and what you will see.

📝
Teacher Appraisal
Admin · Appraisal · /teacher-self-appraisal
Open
What it is
Your self-appraisal form. You rate yourself against the Eduversal Appraisal Framework (each focus area, 1–4) and add evidence.
When to use it
Before your post-conference meeting with your appraiser. Fill it in honestly so the discussion is balanced.
What you see
  • The framework's focus areas, each with a 1–4 rubric.
  • A score selector and an evidence note per area.
  • Cambridge Teacher Standards CTS chips you can click for the source.
  • A save / submit button.
Good to know
Your scores and evidence are visible to your appraiser alongside their observation scores. It is your voice in the room — use it.
🏅
Appraisal Results
Admin · Appraisal · /teacher-appraisal-results
Open
What it is
A read-only view of your finalised appraisal — the agreed score per focus area, your composite, your A–F band, and growth goals.
When to use it
After your post-conference, once your appraiser has finalised the record. Until then it may say the result is not ready yet.
What you see
  • Your score per focus area + the weighted composite.
  • Your overall A–F band.
  • Growth goals — your agreed next steps.
  • History of past years, if any.
Good to know
This page is for reading. If a score surprises you, talk to your appraiser — the result is agreed in the post-conference, not changed afterwards here.

A soft list, not a test. These keep your appraisal stress-free.

1
Read the framework early
Open Teacher Appraisal at the start of the year. Knowing the focus areas helps you collect evidence as you go.
2
Keep examples through the year
A lesson that went well, a piece of student feedback, a project you tried. These become your evidence at self-appraisal time.
3
Score yourself fairly
An honest self-appraisal makes the post-conference a real conversation. Over- or under-scoring both make it harder.
4
Use the result to plan
After the cycle, open Appraisal Results and act on the growth goals. They point to your next focus — and link nicely to your Competency Framework work.
Tip: The same good teaching shows up in your Appraisal, your KPI, and your Competency Framework. Doing the work once feeds all three — but each is scored separately.

Short, plain meanings for the terms in the Appraisal pages.

Appraisalyearly review
A once-a-year review of your teaching against a shared framework. Your school principal leads it, often after watching lessons.
Self-Appraisalyour own version
When you rate yourself first, before the official version. It is your voice in the conversation.
Appraiseryour reviewer
The person who observes you and agrees your final score — usually your school principal, working on Academic Hub.
Walkthrough / Observationlesson visit
A short, structured visit to your lesson by your appraiser. It gathers evidence for the appraisal.
Post-conferencethe discussion
The meeting where you and your appraiser compare your self-scores with theirs and agree the final result.
Composite & bandyour overall result
The focus-area scores combine into one composite number, which maps to an overall A–F band.
Evidenceyour examples
Short notes or examples that back up your self-score for a focus area.
CTSCambridge Teacher Standards 2023
The Cambridge document the appraisal framework anchors to. A tag like CTS 4.1 means standard 4.1. Click the chip to read the source.
